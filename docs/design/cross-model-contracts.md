# Cross-model contracts: how much we can support, and how to keep it rigorous

Status: design notes, theory-first. The rigor model and the provability story are the settled parts; the authoring surface is deliberately deferred until they are pinned, and the open questions at the end mark what a real project still has to decide.

Audience: anyone reasoning about what a contract spanning more than one model can assert and how dblect backs it, and anyone building the detectors, certificates, and generators that would. It assumes the substrate in [`lineage-facts.md`](lineage-facts.md), the propagation calculus in [`propagation-soundness.md`](propagation-soundness.md), the nullability treatment in [`nullability-hazards.md`](nullability-hazards.md), the type algebra in [`domain-type-algebra.md`](domain-type-algebra.md), the generator in [`contract-directed-generation.md`](contract-directed-generation.md), and the authoring surface in [`declaration-dsl.md`](declaration-dsl.md). The contract vocabulary it points at is introduced in the [technical intro](dblect_technical_intro.md).

Aggregate conservation, a rollup that should tie out to its source, is the worked example throughout, because it exercises every part of the rigor model at once. The general claim is in [How much cross-model contract, and how to keep it rigorous](#how-much-cross-model-contract-and-how-to-keep-it-rigorous).

## The bug, and why it is a nullability bug

A common worry an analytics team wants written down is that the revenue rolled up by zip in `rev_by_zip` equals the revenue in the `orders` it came from. The motivating failure is concrete, and it is a nullability bug wearing a conservation costume. `zip_code` can be null on an order whose `country` is not, so a rollup that groups by `zip_code` gathers the null-zip orders into one phantom group, and a downstream join on zip drops that group because null never equals null. The null-zip revenue vanishes and the per-zip total stops adding up to its source. Writing the rollup as `where zip_code is not null` drops the same revenue one step earlier.

Before designing how an author would *write* such a contract, the load-bearing question is what the framework can prove or demonstrate about it, because the answer needs very little new authoring surface. Conservation is not a new primitive; it is a theorem whose side conditions are properties the substrate already propagates.

## Conservation is a conjunction of properties we already track

If `rev_by_zip` is the group-by-`zip` sum-of-`revenue` rollup of `orders`, the grand-total identity `sum(rev_by_zip.revenue) == sum(orders.revenue)` is the associativity of `+` over a partition of the rows. It holds under four side conditions, and each is a property the framework already carries or is building:

- **The partition is total and survives.** Every source row lands in exactly one group that is retained downstream. This is where the example breaks: a nullable group key quarantines the null rows into a NULL group that a later join or filter drops. Discharged by the **nullability** property on the group key (a proven `NON_NULL` key keeps the partition total) and refuted by a proven `NULLABLE` key whose null group is dropped.
- **No value is counted twice.** `revenue` is summed at a grain where each order contributes once; a join that fans `orders` out replicates it. Discharged by the **uniqueness and grain** property, which already powers the fan-out finding.
- **The filters agree.** Any `where` on the rollup side matches the rows reconciled on the source side. Predicate equivalence is undecidable in general and tractable on the boolean-comparison-and-null-test fragment the **predicate engine** already parses for conditional-fact activation.
- **The tag is coherent.** Every contributing `revenue` is one currency, the **domain-type** property from the [composition rules](declaration-dsl.md#composition-where-the-types-earn-their-keep).

So conservation is provable exactly when all four side conditions are discharged from the existing properties, and a *hazard* is provable when any one is provably violated. The example is the first condition failing, which is to say it is a nullability-through-aggregation question. That reframing is the reason a dedicated aggregate-contract DSL is mostly not the thing to build first: the headline bug is already a hazard the nullability property is meant to raise, with no contract written at all.

## What the existing nullity detection proves today, and what it cannot yet

Grounding this in the code rather than the wish: the nullability property is a tri-state `{NON_NULL, NULLABLE, UNKNOWN}` on a precision lattice, and it follows the firewall that a hazard fires only on a *proven* `NULLABLE`, never on the nullable-by-default `UNKNOWN`, the abstract-interpretation posture of falling back to top under uncertainty (Cousot and Cousot). `NULLABLE` is a first-class value the lattice meets, the semiring taints through, and grounding folds exactly as it folds a `NON_NULL`. Two layers of detection already consume it:

- **Locally, the structural detectors fire on a single model's AST.** `detect_null_group_after_outer_join` flags a `GROUP BY` over the optional side of an outer join in the same `SELECT`, the within-one-model version of the example; `detect_coalesce_on_join_key` and `detect_where_on_outer_joined_nullable` cover the neighboring shapes. They are a broad, false-positive-tolerant net.
- **Cross-model, the property-based detectors fire on inherited nullability.** `detect_null_group_on_nullable_key` consumes a column the property proved `NULLABLE` upstream and flags the `GROUP BY` that gathers its nulls into a phantom group, complemented by `detect_join_on_nullable_key` and `detect_not_in_nullable_subquery`. These are the case the structural net cannot see: the nullability rode in from a `LEFT JOIN` several models back, and the local SQL shows an innocent column. They rest on `NULLABLE` introducers that are built and wired, the outer-join optional side (a taint rewrite over the relation graph), `NULLIF`, and the `NULL` literal, with the conditional `not_null` activation as the clearing guard that keeps them quiet on healthy models. So the zip example, when `zip_code` is nullable because of an upstream outer join, is caught today, statically, across model boundaries, with provenance naming where the null entered. The catalog and the firewall are worked through in [nullability-hazards.md](nullability-hazards.md).

What is *not* yet emitting `NULLABLE` is the rest of the source catalog: an `ELSE`-less `CASE`, and the declared nullable flag the substrate's discoverer table anticipates alongside `not_null`, which is how the framework would infer that a column is not-`not_null` and therefore nullable. Until those are called out, a column nullable for a reason other than the built introducers rests at `UNKNOWN` and the detectors stay silent on it, which is the firewall being conservative rather than a gap in the machinery.

So "what can we prove" has a precise answer: today, the local null-group shape structurally, and the cross-model inherited shape whenever the nullability traces to an outer join, `NULLIF`, or a `NULL` literal, soundly and with provenance; widening to the remaining `NULLABLE` sources is a matter of calling them out. Neither catch needs an authored aggregate contract.

## How we know: static proof versus empirical demonstration

A contract is a specification, and dblect verifies it two ways. These are different epistemic acts, and keeping them distinct is what keeps the framework honest.

- **Static proof is conservative and one-sided.** It can prove a side condition holds (`NON_NULL` key, key preserved through the join, one filter implies the other), prove one is violated, or stay silent. It never asserts conservation *holds* on faith; an `UNKNOWN` key produces no finding. We know it is sound because each property is a monotone analysis that tops out under uncertainty, and the nullability soundness obligations are already written down: only proven `NULLABLE` fires, the outer-join introducer marks only the genuinely optional side, every clearing guard is honored. Its reward is certainty over all inputs, at PR time, with no data.
- **The property-based testing loop is the general verifier.** It turns the contract into generated inputs, runs the model on them, and shrinks any failure to a minimal counterexample. The NullKey and EmptyGroup intents ([contract-directed-generation.md](contract-directed-generation.md)) manufacture exactly the null-zip order and the group present on one side but not the other, so a tie-out failure surfaces with a shrunk three-row example. A pass is "not refuted by the shapes we tried" rather than a proof, the trust class the framework attaches to anything it checks rather than proves. This is the half that makes dblect a testing tool and not only an analyzer, and it reaches what static proof cannot: an opaque operator, a value-level fact, an independent pipeline.

The two are complementary, not ranked. Static proof closes a decidable fragment outright; the testing loop covers the rest and independently exercises even the proven cases against data. Both catch the example with no authored contract, the generated null key and the existing hazard each demonstrating the row loss on their own.

## How much cross-model contract, and how to keep it rigorous

Conservation is one instance of the general question: across models, what can a contract assert and still be backed the way the rest of the framework backs its claims? The framework's rigor has a definite shape, and a cross-model claim earns the name "contract" only when it fits that shape. Two guarantees define it. Every claim is either propagated as a lattice-valued property along lineage and discharged conservatively, topping out under uncertainty rather than guessing (Cousot and Cousot), or it carries an explicit trust class that records how it was verified. Nothing is reported as known without one of those backings. That gives a clean admissibility test, and it sorts cross-model contracts by *how they are verified* rather than by what they say.

### Contracts that are properties: propagate them

The strongest cross-model contracts are themselves properties, a value on a lattice the substrate already carries along lineage: a domain type, nullability, uniqueness, grain (the counting semiring), a functional dependency, referential integrity. These are cross-model for free, because propagation is cross-model. The fact enters where it is grounded and the finding fires wherever the propagated fact meets an operation that violates it, model boundaries notwithstanding. This is the bulk of what we can and should support, and the rigor is inherited wholesale: lattice and semiring laws under property tests, an empirical soundness oracle against a warehouse (the uniqueness and nullability soundness PBTs are the template), and the boundary discipline that separates a proven value from a vouched one.

The frontier here is the functional dependency, because it turns a class of value questions into propagation. `country -> currency`, declared where it holds and propagated through projections and joins, discharges the currency-coherence obligation on an aggregation many models away, and because it is itself checkable, a row that violates it becomes its own finding rather than a silent license. It is uniqueness generalized from "this set determines everything" to "this set determines that set," so it extends the carrier the substrate already runs; [functional-dependencies.md](functional-dependencies.md) sketches the algebra, the transfer rules, and the discharge. Adding propagating properties (functional dependencies, cardinality, completeness) is the highest-leverage way to grow cross-model coverage, since each one compounds on the engine that already exists.

### Contracts that are relations between aggregates: certify or demonstrate

The second kind does not propagate, because it relates two quantities that meet only at the comparison: `sum(A.x) == sum(B.y)`, a ratio, an inequality. There is no single lineage path to carry such a claim. The testing loop verifies it by default, and a static certificate is a sound fast-path for the fragment where one exists; the framework should say which backing a given check earned.

- **Demonstrated empirically (the default).** The contract-directed generator manufactures inputs that target its known failure shapes (NullKey, Fanout, Orphan, EmptyGroup), runs the models, and shrinks any failure to a minimal counterexample. Its rigor is that of property-based testing (Claessen and Hughes): a pass is "not refuted by the shapes we tried," and the report carries that trust class. The soundness obligation sits on the generator and the oracle, the obligation the uniqueness and nullability soundness PBTs already discharge. This works for any contract whose two sides we can render and run, which is nearly all of them.
- **Certified statically (the fast-path).** When both sides reduce, through non-opaque operators, to relational-algebra terms over a common ancestor, the equality can be proven by an algebraic certificate without running anything: a sum distributes over a partition, a filter on one side is implied by a filter on the other, a key is preserved across a join. Read through provenance semirings (Green, Karvounarakis, and Tannen), these are one statement, that the two sides carry equal annotations in the counting semiring, which is the same `N`-semiring the substrate already uses for fan-out. So the no-double-count condition and the total-partition condition are the same algebraic fact, and the engine for both is the lineage normaliser plus the predicate-implication checker. This is the aggregate-query-equivalence problem (Cohen, Nutt, and Sagiv; Chaudhuri and Vardi for bag semantics), decidable on a fragment and undecidable in general, which fixes the honest scope: certificates exist for derivations over selection, projection, join, union, and group-by, and the moment a side passes through a window region, a recursive CTE, or a UDF (the type-erasing boundaries the substrate already marks), the certificate is unavailable and the check stands on the demonstrated backing alone.

The admissibility line falls here. A claim that is neither a property, nor certifiable, nor soundly generatable-and-checkable is an assertion with no verification mode, and the framework should not dress it as a contract. The `@contract.check` escape hatch is exactly that weakest class, kept visible and rare so its lighter backing is never mistaken for the others.

### What this asks of the DSL

The authoring surface's job is to keep claims inside those backings and to make the backing legible. Four rules follow.

- **Claims are symbolic over typed proxies.** A contract body builds an AST over column proxies that carry their `DomainType`s, the shape the contract methods already use. One expression is then type-checked, normalised for a certificate, and rendered to SQL for the oracle. A raw SQL string forfeits the types and the certificate; a SQL-flavored surface is admissible precisely when it parses into the same typed AST, and sqlglot is the natural parser since the framework already depends on it. The choice among spellings is settled by which preserves the analyzable, typed term, and the proxy expression is the default because it always does.
- **A closed vocabulary of verbs, plus one marked escape.** Each verb (`conservation`, `cardinality`, `functional_dependency`, `referential_integrity`) names a known algebra and a known oracle, which is how the surface stays inside a decidable or generatable fragment. `@contract.check` is the open-world valve, and its findings wear the weakest trust class.
- **Degrade gracefully, and say so.** A `conservation` whose two sides the normaliser cannot relate does not error; it falls from certified to demonstrated, and the finding reports which backing it earned. The trust class is shown, the same provenance discipline the substrate keeps between a proven and a vouched value.
- **A contract lives where its fact holds.** A claim attaches to the relation that grounds it and propagates from there; a genuinely ownerless cross-model invariant is a standalone declaration. Authoring convenience does not move a fact to a model where it cannot be checked.

So the answer to how much is: as much as reduces to a propagating property or to a certifiable-or-generatable relation, which is a large and principled territory, and no more. The way it stays rigorous is that every cross-model contract inherits one of the framework's existing backings, a sound lattice propagation, an algebraic certificate, or a property-based oracle, and the report always names which.

## The authoring surface, deferred

Given the properties and the testing loop catch the null-leak unaided, an authored aggregate contract is justified by the residue they cannot reach: a tie-out between two relations that are *not* a derivation of one another, independent pipelines that should agree, where there is no partition theorem to lean on and only data can confirm the equality. The vocabulary for it already exists in the [technical intro](dblect_technical_intro.md) as `@contract.conservation` and the standalone reconciliation contract; the point here is that it is a thin layer over the properties, not the foundation.

### A green check means three different things

When a contract does relate two aggregates, what a passing check is worth depends on how the two relations relate through lineage, and the report should name the regime rather than collapse three assurances into one mark:

- **Derivation-provable.** The left relation is a group-by rollup of the right over the same rows, so the identity is the partition theorem above, proven statically with zero tolerance and refuted the instant a change breaks a side condition. This is the aggregate-consistency reasoning a materialized-view maintainer does (Gupta and Mumick survey the line of work).
- **Type-and-grain checkable.** The two relations are independent, so the value needs data, but the contract is still statically meaningful or it is not: both sides must aggregate compatible domain types (a `MoneyUSD` total against a `MoneyEUR` total is a finding, not a reconciliation), each side's own aggregation must be sound, and the grains must line up. Catching "these two totals are not the same kind of thing" needs no data.
- **Runtime-confirmed.** The tie-out itself, under real or generated data, within tolerance, through the generator shapes above. A pass is as good as the data exercised, no stronger.

### Cliffs to mark so the surface stays small

- **Tolerance is meaning, not a mute button.** A `tolerance` is the rounding and timing budget the equality genuinely allows, and the derivation regime needs none because it is exact. A tolerance that grows to silence a real drift is the smell; a contract whose tolerance must widen over time is reporting a bug rather than absorbing one.
- **Levels do not sum over time.** Reconciling a level measure (a balance, inventory on hand, headcount) by summing it across time is the semi-additive trap, where the figure means nothing even though the arithmetic checks. An aggregate contract over a level must respect the dimensions the measure is additive over, the third aggregation precondition in [domain-type-algebra.md](domain-type-algebra.md).

### Why the spelling waits

The reason to defer the authoring spelling is methodological. The nullability side condition is already live cross-model; until the remaining three (no fan-out, agreeing filters, a coherent tag) are each pinned to a property with its soundness obligation at the aggregation site, we do not know which residue the DSL must cover or what a green check would honestly mean. The theory fixes the scope; the surface follows it.

## Open questions

- **Widening the `NULLABLE` sources.** The cross-model catch is live for nullability that traces to an outer join, `NULLIF`, or a `NULL` literal. The remaining sources, an `ELSE`-less `CASE` and the declared nullable flag that reads a column as not-`not_null`, are the call-outs that would extend the same detectors to columns nullable for those reasons. Tracked in [nullability-hazards.md](nullability-hazards.md).
- **Bare phantom group versus downstream-dropped.** `detect_null_group_on_nullable_key` fires on the nullable group key itself; the lost-money version is when that phantom group is later dropped through a join. Whether to fire on the bare group or wait for the downstream drop is an open question in [nullability-hazards.md](nullability-hazards.md), and it is exactly the line between a curiosity and a conservation failure.
- **What a generic assertion DSL is written in.** A broader assertion than conservation is welcome if the testing loop evaluates it, and the language is the open part. Raw SQL is universal and portable, at the cost of being stringy and of forfeiting dblect's types unless re-parsed. A Python expression over our column proxies natively carries the `DomainType`s, so the assertion typechecks and gains tag and grain coherence for free, against the open-ended complexity the `@contract.check` escape hatch already flags. A SQL-flavored surface parsed into a sqlglot AST is a middle path, a familiar syntax that still yields the structured, typed tree the framework already walks everywhere. The proxy-expression form is the natural default, since it reuses the contract-method machinery and keeps the types, but the choice is unsettled and best made against real assertions.
- **How large a predicate fragment to recognize.** The filter-agreement side condition is decidable only on a fragment, and how far to push recognition before falling back to a runtime check wants a real reconciliation mart to size against. The conditional-fact activation engine already fixes the starting fragment.
- **Whether the trust regime is shown to the author or kept internal.** Surfacing "proven" versus "tied out on the data we ran" in the finding is more honest; keeping it internal provenance is simpler. This is the same trust-visibility question the `Field` split raises in [declaration-dsl.md](declaration-dsl.md).
- **One finding or two when the aggregate is unsound and the output type is also wrong.** When a sum mixes currencies and the result is assigned a single-currency type, both statements are true. Which to make primary is a diagnostics call that wants real output in front of real users.

## Prior art

The conservation hazards are the well-charted surprises of SQL's three-valued logic, with Date's critiques of nulls the classic reference for why grouping and joining on a nullable column behave as they do. The static side is ordinary abstract interpretation, a sound monotone analysis that falls back to top under uncertainty (Cousot and Cousot). The derivation-provable regime is the aggregate-consistency reasoning of materialized-view maintenance (Gupta and Mumick). The cross-model reach rests on the provenance line the substrate already draws on, where-provenance from Cheney, Chiticariu, and Tan and the propagation calculus from Green, Karvounarakis, and Tannen. Reconciliation tests themselves are long-standing analytics practice, encoded in `dbt_utils.equality`, audit_helper, and the assertion vocabularies of the data-quality tools; dblect's contribution is to type the two sides, to prove the derivable cases statically, and to demonstrate the rest with adversarially generated shapes.
